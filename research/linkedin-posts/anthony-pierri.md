## Post 1
Founders think if they JUST launch this next feature, they'll be successful. Everyone knows to avoid the famous "feature trap," and it comes from a good place. It's way easier for founders to spend time building features than it is to do the hard work of talking to customers and figuring out distribution. The cost of engineering is way less than the cost of distribution (due mainly to AI). So it's always a temptation to just focus on shipping new features as a way to feel productive without tackling the real problems. But the flip side is that it downplays the role of design and user experience. There are several insanely successful products that became known because of ONE specific feature. This feature was often the difference between finding product-market-fit or dying a slow and painful death.

## Post 2
I'm not saying all marketers are liars, but a lot of what we do is promise things that go far beyond what we can guarantee. We've been trained to "sell the outcome." And in business contexts that means selling the business outcome. Things like "getting more customers," "increasing revenue," "business growth." Here's the problem: business outcomes are never driven by one thing. Every business outcome is determined by an interlocking set of factors that span the entire business. No product can guarantee a business-level outcome. To tell someone that adopting a CRM will make them "win more customers" is simply a lie. A rule of thumb: if you would feel very uncomfortable putting "increase revenue" in your contract as a guaranteed outcome, maybe avoid saying it in your marketing materials.

## Post 3
Look at your homepage and see how you answer this question: "what is your product?" Do you at least say what product category you're in? Or do you at least list the main use case of your product? And are both of these specific enough that your ICP would immediately "get it"? It may surprise you to find how few companies actually pass this simple test. You will find far more answering with outcomes-only or vision-only. If you don't adequately answer "what is it," then no one will care about anything else you have to say.
